Improving land surface phenology retrievals for arid and semi-arid ecosystems

MCD12Q2 represents a significant data product sourced from the Terra and Aqua combined Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS). It has been developed to offer a global depiction of land surface phenology metrics at yearly intervals. The most recent iteration, MCD12Q2 Version 6.1, presents data from 2001 to 2021. The data product is developed from the processing of time-series comprising the two-band Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI2), which is derived from MODIS Nadir Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function-Adjusted Reflectance (NBAR). It has been designed to identify vegetation phenology metrics at 500-meter spatial resolution, including up to two observed growing cycles per year.

Despite its global coverage, the MCD12Q2 product has certain limitations, as it does provide sufficient information for dryland ecosystems. Specifically, MCD12Q2 was only available for two years out of an eight-year observation period for a particular site where ground measurements were available. Through an analysis of ground measurements taken from the same location, it was clear that vegetation and photosynthesis were taking place at the same site where MCD12Q2 failed to produce any data.

This failure to produce data was not attributable to the lack of vegetation. Rather, MCD12Q2 employs a conservative approach in measuring phenological metrics, whereby a threshold of 0.1 is set for the seasonal amplitude (i.e., the difference between EVI max and EVI min values). Consequently, when the EVI's amplitude falls below this threshold, MCD12Q2 generates no data to avoid capturing random noise. Unfortunately, this approach results in a lack of data from regions with low vegetation index amplitude, such as drylands.

Research Question Can the detection of vegetation phenology in arid regions be improved by customizing the current global threshold of 0.1 to a more regionally appropriate value?

The present study is focused on six arid regions located within the United States, carefully selected due to the availability of ground-based measurements of photosynthesis. The primary data sources employed in this project are the MODIS MYD13A1 product, which provide EVI metrics, and flux measurements for photosynthesis. These data sources will be used for the purpose of validating the study's findings.

In this study, the MCD12Q2 approach is being largely followed for data preprocessing, which includes the elimination of errors and the application of a penalizing cubic smoothing spline to the time series. phenometrics are being defined in a similar way as MCD12Q2. However, there are three notable differences in the approach. Two of these differences are minor, but the most significant difference is the application of pixel-wise seasonal amplitude restrictions to capture a wider range of growing seasons with varying amplitudes. This approach differs from MCD12Q2's use of the same amplitude restriction for all pixels. The results of the preliminary analysis for phenometrics using a new regional pixel approach for the year 2007, where MCD12Q2 provided no data, are presented in this section. By implementing this novel approach, it was possible to determine the timing of the seven phenometrics (Figure 4). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the regional pixel approach in identifying phenological metrics in areas where traditional approaches may not provide sufficient data. Further research is needed to validate these results and extent the approach to other regions and time periods.

Figure 4 shows the results of anlaysis using a novel regional pixel approach for the seven phenometrics (Greenup, Mid-greenup, maturity, peak, senescence, mid-greendown, and dormancy) in the year 2007, where MCD12Q2 data was unavailable.

The next steps in this study involve verifying the accuracy of the phenometrics dates obtained through the new regional pixel approach. To achieve this, a comparison will be made between the dates retrieved and those obtained from flux sites. Despite this, initial observations suggest that the EVI values obtained through the new approach visually correspond well with those obtained from the flux sites, as demonstrated by the figure presented (Figure 5). Once the validation process is complete, the analysis will be extended to include drylands across the world, including regions where flux sites are not available. This expansion of the study will provide a more comprehensive understanding of phenometrics and their relationship with environmental factors in drylands globally.
